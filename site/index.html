How It Works

Methodology

Illegal parking in Bengaluru is a significant but underquantified contributor to road congestion. When a vehicle parks illegally on a main road or near an intersection, it effectively narrows the usable lane width, forcing traffic to slow, merge, and queue. Enforcement today is largely reactive — officers respond to complaints or patrol fixed routes — meaning the most problematic zones may not receive proportional attention relative to their actual impact.

Our approach starts with a key insight: BTP already maintains a named junction taxonomy that officers use while recording violations. Rather than discarding that domain knowledge, Pipeline B keeps those known junction records intact and focuses discovery work on the remaining "No Junction" records. For that remainder, we apply street-grouped HDBSCAN — grouping records by the street name extracted from the address field before clustering, so density-based clustering operates within a single road rather than chaining across spatially adjacent but functionally separate streets. This prevents the common failure mode where a continuous road network collapses into one artificially large false cluster.

Each identified hotspot — whether a confirmed BTP junction or a newly discovered unofficial one — is then scored on four dimensions: raw violation frequency (how much activity?), a congestion-severity weighted score (are the violation types the kind that block traffic, like double parking on a main road, versus non-congestion violations like defective number plates?), peak-hour concentration (are violations happening during congestion windows or off-peak?), and average vehicle footprint weight (a heavy goods vehicle parked illegally consumes far more road capacity than a parked motorcycle). These four components are min-max normalized and combined with a confidence discount for low-volume hotspots, producing a single Impact Score (0–100) that can directly prioritize enforcement resources.

1

Junction-First Split & Footprint Reclamation

Records with a BTP junction name are aggregated directly. Footprint Reclamation: "No Junction" records falling within a confirmed junction's 95th-percentile spatial radius are reassigned to that junction, correcting labeling inconsistencies in the source data as a built-in Pipeline B capability.

2

Street-Grouped HDBSCAN

Remaining "No Junction" records are clustered per street name, preventing cross-street spatial chaining while still surfacing unofficial corridors and small local hotspots.

3

Distribution Enrichment

Per-hotspot violation type and vehicle type breakdowns computed. Congestion-relevance and severity weights applied.

4

Impact Scoring

Four normalized components combined (30% volume + 30% severity + 20% peak-hour + 20% footprint) with confidence discount for low-sample hotspots.

5

Repeat Offender & Station Rollup

Vehicle-level recidivism analysis and police station workload are summarized after hotspot scoring to support downstream enforcement planning.
